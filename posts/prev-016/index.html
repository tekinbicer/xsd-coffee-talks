Exceptional fossils: feathers, soft tissues, and ancient molecules

Posted at — Sep 5, 2019

Abstract

Within the last two to three decades, the field of paleontology has undergone some radical shifts. The application of new analytical equipment and experimental approaches, along with new fossil discoveries, have not only changed our understanding of life in the past, but also how we test hypotheses in a traditionally historical science. In relation to my work, the discovery of exceptionally preserved dinosaur skeletons with feathers has provided new insight into the evolution of birds and flight. Preservation of soft tissues have also opened new avenues of investigation, such as pigment detection and color reconstruction of dinosaurs. My work focuses not just on fossil feathers, but on how soft tissues preserve in fossils more generally. This involves collaboration with various chemists, physicists, and engineers as we seek to detect ancient biomolecules and understand how they preserve. Currently, I am collaborating with Argonne to investigate trace metal accumulation in carbonaceous fossil soft tissues using synchrotron XRF in order to determine what these signatures can, or possibly can't, tell us about the fossil record.

Bio

Evan Saitta is a post-doctoral researcher at the Field Museum of Natural History in the Integrative Research Center, Section of Earth Sciences. He received his Ph.D. in Geology and M.Sc. in Paleobiology from the University of Bristol in the UK. He studied his B.A. in Ecology and Evolutionary Biology at Princeton University.
